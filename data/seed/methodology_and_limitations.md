# Surveillance Methodology and Its Limitations

> **SYNTHETIC DATA.** This document was generated by `scripts/build_dataset.py` for development and grading. The figures are plausible in shape but fabricated. Do not cite them. See data/README.md for how to substitute real WHO GLASS / ECDC EARS-Net data.


## Denominators

Reported percentages are non-susceptible isolates divided by tested isolates.
This denominator is not the population — it is the set of isolates a clinician
chose to culture and a laboratory chose to test. Testing propensity varies with
health-system capacity, so a region that cultures more readily will report
different figures from one that cultures only after empirical failure, even
with identical underlying epidemiology.

## Comparability between regions

Networks differ in breakpoint standard (EUCAST versus CLSI), in the mix of
participating hospital types, and in whether repeat isolates from the same
patient are deduplicated. Between-region differences of a few percentage points
should not be treated as real. Within-region trends over time are considerably
more reliable, because the methodological differences are approximately
constant across years within one network.

## Lag

Surveillance data are published with a lag of twelve to twenty-four months. Any
statement about "current" resistance derived from published surveillance is a
statement about the situation one to two years ago. During periods of rapid
change — the post-2020 period in several networks — this lag is material.

## Small denominators

Where fewer than approximately 300 isolates were tested, the confidence interval
around a reported percentage is wide enough that year-on-year movements of
several percentage points carry little information. Series with small
denominators should be read as levels, not as trends.
