## The threshold principle

Standard guidance holds that an agent should not be used empirically for a
syndrome when local non-susceptibility for the likely pathogen exceeds
approximately 10-20%, the exact threshold depending on the severity of the
syndrome and the cost of an inadequate first dose. For septic shock the
tolerance is lower, because the mortality cost of 24 hours of inadequate
therapy is high; for uncomplicated urinary infection it is higher, because the
cost of failure is a change of agent rather than a death.

## Applying thresholds to surveillance data

Two cautions apply when reading regional surveillance figures against these
thresholds.

First, regional aggregates conceal hospital-level variation. A region reporting
20% carbapenem non-susceptibility in Klebsiella pneumoniae will contain
tertiary centres well above that figure and community hospitals well below it.
Empirical guidance should be set on local antibiogram data where it exists;
regional figures are a fallback, not a substitute.

Second, invasive-isolate surveillance oversamples severe disease. Percentages
derived from blood and CSF isolates are systematically higher than those that
would be observed across all clinical specimens, so applying an invasive-isolate
percentage to an outpatient syndrome overestimates risk.

## Escalation and de-escalation

Where regional carbapenem non-susceptibility in Enterobacterales exceeds 15%,
reporting networks describe a shift toward carbapenem-sparing empirical regimens
for severe hospital-acquired infection, with reservation of carbapenems for
culture-confirmed cases. De-escalation within 48-72 hours of culture results is
described as the single most important lever for containing selective pressure,
and the one most often omitted under workload pressure.
