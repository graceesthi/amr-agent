## Priority tiers

The pathogens tracked in this corpus map onto the standard priority framing as
follows. Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii and carbapenem-resistant
Enterobacterales — chiefly Klebsiella pneumoniae and Escherichia coli — sit in
the critical tier, on the grounds that they cause severe hospital-acquired
infection and the remaining treatment options are few, toxic, or both.
Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus faecium and meticillin-resistant
Staphylococcus aureus sit in the high tier.

## What the tiering is for

The tiers are a research-and-development prioritisation instrument, not a
clinical risk score. A pathogen is critical-tier because the pipeline of new
agents against it is thin, not because an individual infection with it is
necessarily more dangerous than one with a high-tier organism. Stewardship
committees that read the tiers as clinical severity rankings misallocate
attention.

## Data gaps that the framing exposes

Surveillance coverage is markedly weaker for the critical-tier Gram-negatives in
low- and middle-income settings than in the European networks, which means the
global figures for those organisms are dominated by the regions that report
best. Statements about global trends in carbapenem resistance are therefore
statements about well-surveilled regions, and the direction of the bias is
unknown rather than conservative.
