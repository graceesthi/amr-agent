## Prospective audit and feedback

Prospective audit with direct prescriber feedback remains the intervention with
the most consistent effect on carbapenem consumption in the reporting networks.
Sites running weekly audit rounds reported a median reduction of 18% in
carbapenem defined daily doses within twelve months of implementation. The
effect attenuates when audit frequency drops below fortnightly, which suggests
the mechanism is sustained prescriber contact rather than the audit artefact
itself.

Importantly, reduced consumption did not translate into reduced resistance
within the same twelve-month window in any reporting site. Resistance responds
on a multi-year lag, and stewardship programmes evaluated on one-year
resistance endpoints will appear to fail even when they are working.

## Preauthorisation

Formulary restriction requiring infectious-disease approval before release of
carbapenems and last-line agents produced faster consumption drops than audit
and feedback — a median 27% reduction — but with two documented costs: delayed
first doses in sepsis pathways where the approval step sat on the critical
path, and substitution toward unrestricted broad-spectrum agents, which shifted
rather than reduced selective pressure.

## Rapid diagnostics

Molecular rapid diagnostic panels shortened time to organism identification by a
median of 26 hours. Sites that paired rapid diagnostics with an active
stewardship response reported meaningful de-escalation; sites that deployed the
panels without a response pathway reported no change in prescribing. The
diagnostic is not the intervention — the response to it is.

## What does not work

Passive education campaigns, guideline circulation without enforcement, and
one-off audit exercises showed no measurable effect on consumption in any
reporting network.
